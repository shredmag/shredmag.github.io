PLASTIC STINKS!

The myth of recyclable plastic cosmetics and sustainable alternatives





















There’s no avoiding advertising. 


It’s on the walls when you walk around town; it’s integrated into your music and podcasts; it’s all you see on social media. You wouldn’t be alone if you had a drawer full of old – or new – skincare and makeup products. 


We can’t help but be influenced when it’s so ingrained into our society. So, to try ‘de-influencing’ myself, I went through some of my cosmetics to figure out what could actually be recycled.


One of my old favourites for facial cleansers is the CeraVe Foaming Cleanser. Thanks to ‘SkinTok’, the skincare side of TikTok, it’s become considered a necessity for oily-combination skin folks like myself. 


However, pumps in cosmetic packaging are incredibly hard to recycle. They contain many small moving parts, often made of different materials that can’t be recycled together. Parts that are smaller than 8cm can clog machines as noted by senior director of social change, behaviour, and impact at The Recycling Partnership Elizabeth Schussler in an interview with Coveteur. 


It’s easier to just reuse this packaging. For example, I’m slowly refilling my pump bottle with a refill bag of hand soap while using a face wash that doesn’t have a pump package. 


As the days get colder, I find myself reaching for my lip balms more often. In particular, I loved the e.l.f Bite Size Lip Balm. However, this has the same issue as the pump bottle: the screwing mechanism to make the product move up and down contains a lot of small parts that cannot easily be recycled. 


While you can still mail these to companies like Terracycle that recycle small cosmetics or put them in public recycling bins at places like The Body Shop, I’ve found it easier to instead buy lip balms that come in metal tins. You even get a container to DIY with once it’s finished.

In the modern age, it is instinct to drop any kind of plastic into the recycling bin and close your eyes. You’ve done your part so you get to pat yourself on the back and move on. Unfortunately, it’s becoming more and more apparent that this isn’t the case at all. 


According to MIT, only 9% of all plastic ever produced has been recycled. The mountains and oceans of plastic in landfill and on water aren’t exaggerations. Sometimes these things are sent directly to landfill.


If you want to become a better recycler, remember to thoroughly wash your plastic before you throw it away. This goes for all plastic packaging, cosmetic or otherwise. Run some warm soapy water over and in the packaging at the sink and let it dry so it doesn’t soak any of your cardboard. 


The ideal is to look at alternatives. Try to visit refill stores where you bring your own containers, even reusing some of the plastic ones you already have; use containers that are made of metal that will last longer and be easier to recycle when you finally give it up; or consider packaging-free alternatives like soap bars. 


Don’t worry about what you’ve already bought – just use as much as you can before the expiration date and reuse the containers where you can. Try your hand at painting over old (clean) powder compacts so you can use them as medicine containers or refill your lip balm containers with cute lip tints. Always make the best of what you’ve got.
