TRACY MULLIGAN : WHY SUSTAINABLE FASHION IS THE ONLY WAY FORWARD

One chat with the industry’s most stylish sustainable designer will make you swear off fast fashion forever





























In the past couple of years, there has been a surge of online interest in sustainable fashion. Whether it was born from the guilt created by all those fast fashion lockdown depression hauls is up for debate. From TikTok famous vegan shoe brands to the novelty of a Reformation changing room, it’s all over the internet. 


People Tree did it first. 


Tracy Mulligan, People Tree’s Head of Design from 2008 – 2015 and Creative Director until 2023, was a key figure in setting sustainable trends. 


“I said to them, ‘I really don’t know anything about sustainability’, and they said, ‘Don’t worry, nobody does’. That was in 2008,” she tells me.


Mulligan has been a family friend of mine for years. I can pinpoint my obsession with fashion to two key influences in my life: The Devil Wears Prada and Mulligan herself. I watched the film for the first time with her daughter, glued to their family’s white leather couches. We would flip through magazines, giggling when we found interviews of her mum in 90s Vogues. 





















In 1990, Mulligan graduated from Central Saint Martins. She studied Fashion and Textiles, where there was no ethics training at all in the course. There was no conversation about sustainability and no checking any factories for ethical dilemmas. 


In the early 90s, she co-founded Sonnentag Mulligan and the brand exploded onto the London fashion scene to endless media applause from all over the world.


She later founded her own brand Mulligan in 1998 to critical acclaim. The collections were snapped up by the most fashionable of luxury department stores, including Harvey Nichols and Saks Fifth Avenue.


We flick through her extensive, extremely impressive archive of newspaper cuttings and magazine pages kept in huge portfolio folders. With every page is another well-crafted piece — timeless, simple, structured. Fitted asymmetrical white shirts that would get the Pinterest girls drooling - office siren meets Scandinavian chic. 


Her sleek, well-tailored designs still hold true to today’s trends. Each piece was made by artisans, as all high-end fashion used to be. Individuals would handcraft garments with love, care and expertise. Such well made pieces have become hard to find on the high street.


“While I’ve been living in Surrey, I’ve found it difficult to relate to other mothers who have no interest in where or how their clothes are made,” Mulligan says. “They just want to purchase them at the cheapest price.”


She’s right — the industry has become more and more profit focused and consumers love to pay less. Many brands will use the cheapest materials and the cheapest factories and subject workers to the worst of conditions. In these circumstances, tragedy is inevitable. 


This incessant drive for profit has led to avoidable disasters such as the Rana Plaza factory collapse in Bangladesh. Over 1000 garment workers, mostly women, lost their lives when the factory owners ignored the warnings of unstable, cracked floors and forced workers back into an unsafe environment. The average employee at those five factories reportedly earned only €39 a month.


Some brands still refuse to take accountability. Out of the 29 brands who were known to source garments from the factories in Rana Plaza, only nine went to the meetings about compensating the victims and their families. 


Now that sustainable fashion and secondhand garments are on the rise again, we have to make sure that the focus is on what pieces are made out of and who makes them. A compostable coat has no right to be seen as sustainable if slave labour was used to create it.


Working for People Tree gave Mulligan new insight into the industry she had spent her whole working life in. 


As the first ever Fair Trade certified fashion brand, People Tree is based entirely on working with Fair Trade companies in India, Bangladesh and Nepal. They source 90% of their garments from those organisations, helping to train those in disadvantaged groups, giving them financial stability and safe environments to work in.


“This is the reason I stayed at People Tree for 15 years: to promote better working conditions for people and to use fabrics that are non-toxic for the environment,” Mulligan says. 


“This is true sustainability for me, not the greenwashing that has become fashionable in recent years.”


But of course, the garments have to be stylish too. Mulligan was brought on board at People Tree to revamp their style and widen their demographic.


She recalls the ‘charity shop’ florals and chunky, lumpy sweaters that had the sustainable fashion industry in a chokehold. Stuck in the ‘give peace a chance’ mindset of the earth-conscious, natural-fabric-loving hippies of the sixties, the designers (supplied with fabrics that would not drape) fashioned thick cotton and thicker linen into clothes that could be worn but not flaunted. 


Mulligan changed this. She proved to the industry that it could be done: a garment could be both eco-friendly and elegant. Her ready-to-wear collections were casual and comfortable, with patterns that flattered. People Tree was known for her chic, feminine, wearable designs.



















Green companies, who advocate for ethical sustainability, have to operate on slower timelines and many of them cannot keep up with fickle consumer demands. New and trending items often disappear from potential customers' feeds within the month. Mass production can satiate this rampant demand but at the cost of the planet and everyone on it.


How much longer can we continue feeding the fast fashion monster? Last year alone, the fashion industry pumped out 97 million tonnes of waste. 


Big brands have to have a certain amount of deliveries each week and a designer has to therefore make a certain amount of designs. They've already pre-booked the factories to hit their garment quotas so the deliveries can come into the shops on time. 


All these slots must be filled: the monster needs to be fat and well-fed for the profits to grow. Trends pump out, money comes in, and the people who make the actual clothes are left with pennies.


Tracy Mulligan knows the fashion industry inside and out. She knows it cannot be reformed overnight but talking to her has certainly inspired me to make a significant change in my own life.



MAKING THE CHANGE Q&A:


If a young person was trying to be as sustainable as possible, and didn't have a lot of time or money on their hands, what would be the best things to do?

It would be thrifting. Make sure it’s something that you can resell and if you can't, take it somewhere and recycle it properly. Also, bio-natural fabrics! If you have a coat like that, you can just bury it in the garden or compost it.


Always look out for sales. Put yourself on the mailing list for sustainable brands you like. If you spend a little bit more time making conscious choices then you get the feel-good factor from it too.


And you’re buying something that will last you for longer so you're making a bit of an investment there.

Exactly. Pick natural organic fabrics, look at the swing tags. Some brands are affordable but check the label and see how much of the material is actually sustainable. Always check for genuine certification.


Whatever you can do will make a small difference - try to change your habits, not what you already have. Don’t buy this just to be sustainable, buy it because you need it and you’ve saved up for it.
