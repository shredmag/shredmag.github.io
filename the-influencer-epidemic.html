THE INFLUENCER EPIDEMIC

The newly advertised way of life is the enemy of Earth and your wallet.


The overwhelming need to fulfil every want a person could possibly have without settling for less has caused a giant surge in the market for just about everything, but the people aren’t to blame for wanting what is shoved in their faces.

The root of the problem is firmly planted in overproduction and the fruit of influence that grows from it. The General Multilingual Environmental Thesaurus defines overconsumption to be the excessive use of goods and services to a level that exceeds the planet’s carrying capacity and life supporting abilities – modern consumer culture knows all about that.


Nowadays, the internet is flooded with things influencers insist you need: this anti-tangle brush, this suede shoe cleaner, this leather shoe cleaner, but it doesn’t stop there - because now you get this brown suede shoe cleaner for your brown shoes, obviously.    

 

The epidemic of influencers swarming any and all social media platforms has intensified the average person’s need to purchase to make up for the ‘lacking’ feeling they might get. Ever since we learned that we could have more, we wanted it. Social media nowadays is fashion’s number one source: research shows that 69% of influencers cite social media as their main influence when it comes to their fashion style.


The initial wave of internet gurus grew in popularity because they offered the human connection to products that advertisements in the 90s and 00s lacked. Seeing ‘normal’ people review semi-affordable products as opposed to an untouchable celebrity attracted a new audience that trusted this new group of marketers. A 2019 survey from the MarketingDive revealed that 86% of respondents have bought items because they saw an influencer had it. 


As time went on, the line between movie star and web sensation thinned. This leads us to where we are now: out of touch video creators that swear they are just like you and you need what they have. A TeenVogue article from January 2024 highlights how “influencers no longer feel like trusted friends letting us in on their favourite items, but more like corporations pushing products.” 


A number of scandals on various platforms have taken place in the past two years where creators have been caught lying about certain products to sell them. 


For example, Mikayla Nogueira, a TikTok creator adored for being an unfiltered and funny make-up guru, had a set of false lashes on in a video promoting a mascara brand she claimed “doubled” the length of her natural lashes.

Nogueira was not the first nor the only influencer to be exposed amidst an advertisement. In fact, lying is common for the right price -  a similar rising concern is the influx of health influencers that encourage the use of certain products under the hoax of weight loss or anti-aging benefits, of which a majority were confirmed to be placebos.


Another large factor in the loss of authenticity on the internet is the increase in advertisements on every major social platform. In 2021, it was estimated that the average person would encounter 6,000 to 10,000 ads per day - 12 times more than ad interaction in the 70s. The rapid growth of advertisements in media has catered to society’s need for more. The way that buying things now can be as easy as tapping a few buttons on your phone is representative of product availability being at an all-time high.


Ideas like a new white top being a necessity promotes a herd mentality like no other. In an article for the Harvard Political Review, journalist Nurayn Khan notes the change in the life span of trends today, pointing out that “trend cycles have shortened from 20 years to 10.” According to her, the newly reformed trend cycles distort the consumer's idea of what they should and shouldn’t get. The fact that fast fashion is being replicated within fast fashion is evidence of our rotting perception of trends and the psychological need to be ‘it’. Zara tops are being sold in places like Primark for cheaper, or being sold on sites like Shein or AliExpress in bulk for even cheaper.


In addition to its already limited longevity, the amount of time people actually claim ownership over these purchases cuts itself shorter and shorter as charity shops and thrift stores are being flooded with clothes from above-mentioned brands. Consequently, the gap between prices for second-hand items and retail is closing rapidly - causing people to buy more fast fashion retail just to give it away for no one to buy, once again contributing to an endless cycle of purchase to waste. 


Companies have taken advantage of rapid trend cycles enforced by the influence of the newest viral hack. Shortened production timelines and increased mass production of these items at low costs allows for cheap merchandise. PR packages are a common way that companies make use of this advantage. They send a creator a free package with various items for them to film and advertise - only for these creators to throw them away right after. Additionally, the fancy boxes the products come in are filled with unnecessary packaging made to please the viewer's eye: plastic on styrofoam on plastic, in laminated boxes that go straight to the bins.

The rate at which overconsumption is growing creates an increase in textile waste. The items in demand are often made cheaply with polyester and other non-biodegradable materials that can take decades to decompose. Our planet's resources are already being exhausted at an alarming rate and a surge in demand for products does not help. The impact that influencers have on consumer culture is creating a gap between person and product that convinces buyers that they need more. The level of mass production needed to fulfil our wants now encourages overconsumption which is a major contributor to modern environmental and economical plummet. Not even minimalism is exempt from this harmful cycle as self-proclaimed ‘neat freaks’ purchase an unreasonable amount of boxes, jars, and whatnot just to repackage pre-packaged goods. 

 

https://vt.tiktok.com/ZSFmBxDrc/


While we can’t stop large-scale disposals from big corporations - consuming less, purchasing fewer items, and only investing in the resources we truly need in the long run can greatly decrease the amount of waste we leave behind. An easy and guilt-free way to scratch that retail therapy itch is to practise  mindful maximalism: keep your space loved and full by getting creative with what you use and how you use it. Donations or hand-me-downs are also things you can turn to in order to minimise waste – find the closest charity shop or friend’s apartment. Anything that’s too tattered to give away can easily be reused in so many different ways.
