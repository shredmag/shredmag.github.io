ECOBRICKING - IS IT WORTH IT


”It's such a transforming act. When you see how much plastic is in your daily life…the idea is that the habit makes you more conscious of your daily footprint.” 

- Ian Dommisse, Founder and Vision manager of the Ecobrick Exchange



Ecobricking was introduced in the early years of the 21st century by environmental activist Susana Heisse, in Guatemala. She built a wall from the invention of ecobricks, becoming an inspiration to others around the world and later becoming a system of recycling in itself, with modern technology helping connect fellow ‘ecobrickers’ in trade. Ecobricking is the process of condensing plastic into bottles to create ‘bricks’. They are used to build solid structures like furniture, garden beds and whole buildings. They can even be used as natural insulation due to how tightly packed they are.


It all starts with a bottle of your favourite soft drink or a plastic milk carton on its way out to the bins. You’ll need it to be a non-crushed PET (a form of polyester) plastic bottle. This can be identified by a symbol of a number one in a triangle of arrows, typically placed on the bottom of the bottle. Your average 500ml water bottle is a great place to start. If you are planning to build something from your eco bricks you will need to consider consistency with the type of bottles you use, they will fit together better than bottles of irregular shapes. Sort through your recycling for materials that can be cut up and condensed into the bottles.


Materials that can be used are as such:

-plastic bags

-plastic packaging

-styrofoam

-cellophane

-straws


You’ll be surprised how much rubbish you can fit into just one bottle.


After you’ve collected your plastics, clean and then dry them thoroughly. This will prevent your ecobrick creations from growing mould from leftover traces of food or drinks. The strips or pieces of plastic you have prepared now need to be rammed into the bottle, ensuring that they are compressed enough to keep a firm structure. Some people use a stick, however it is understandable that not everyone has a trusty wooden branch to hand. Alternatives could be a coat hanger or kitchen utensil. Pop the lid back on tightly and admire your first ecobrick.


Platforms like Pinterest or YouTube are full of ideas on how to build with your ecobricks. However, if your aim is to contribute your ecobricks to a larger project, there are multiple ecobrick exchange websites that you can transfer them through. It is important to check the requirements of each ecobrick exchange as they all have different priorities and purposes. You’ll have to find the one that works for you and check that your ecobricks will make the cut!


There are criticisms of the ecobricking method. As they are often used in outdoor projects, the inorganic chemicals that come from plastic can leech into the surrounding environment when exposed to the sun. This causes instant harm to the soil which could make its way to water, threatening aquatic plant and animal life. Microplastics are also a concern. If the bottle collapses or breaks, these will be released into the environment and put animal and human health at risk. There is concern that ecobricks may be a ‘quick fix’ and delay the inevitable action that needs to be taken to offer a long term, sustainable solution to the plastic problem. 


There is no doubt that ecobricks are a straightforward and engaging solution that opens up sustainability to everyone. Schools and communities are engaging in this environmental experiment as a more hands-on approach to primary and secondary education. Children can now be involved in the process of protecting the environment and becoming conscious of plastic waste. Nevertheless, the damage done by excess plastics needs a long term solution, regardless of the extra mile we may all have to go to bring our planet back from the verge.
